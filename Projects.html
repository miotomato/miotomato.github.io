Transcriptional condensates at super-enhancers mediate pH-dependent transcriptional regulation in innate immunity and human cancer

I integrated multiple epigenomics datasets (BRD4, H3K27ac ChIP-seq, ATAC-seq) and transcriptomics (RNA-seq) profiles across multiple mouse macrophage conditions and identified RELA, IRF family, and STAT family transcription factors as candidate regulators of BRD4-associated, pH-sensitive transcriptional condensates.

By integrating epigenomics analysis with TCGA data, I found that putative transcriptional condensate activities in COAD tumor cells are more likely to correlate with poor prognosis, while T-cell-specific transcriptional condensate activities are more likely to associate with better survival.

This work involved genome-wide peak-centric analysis, differential signal modeling, interpretation of pH-sensitive regulatory behavior, and assessment of biological relevance through close coordination with experimental collaborators so that the computational results aligned with mechanistic questions and validation strategies.

Source: bioRxiv | Poster

CTCFexplorer: a systematic framework and comprehensive data resource for CTCF binding in the human and mouse genome

I built CTCFexplorer (www.ctcf.info), an SQL-backed, user-friendly data resource and web platform that curates and processes more than 4,000 public CTCF ChIP-seq datasets from human and mouse into a searchable database. We systematically process these datasets using a robust pipeline that includes metadata curation, quality control, and statistical identification of constitutive and cell-type-specific binding events. The platform is designed to transform large public datasets into practical, accessible scientific resources for researchers.

Source: ctcf.info | Cancer Research | GitHub

Genomic clustering tendency of transcription factors reflects phase-separated transcriptional condensates at super-enhancers

I developed a statistical framework to quantify genome-wide clustering patterns of transcription factor motifs and binding organization. These clustering patterns are significantly associated with super-enhancers. Regions with densely clustered TF binding sites are more enriched at cell-type-specific super-enhancers and exhibit higher chromatin accessibility, elevated chromatin interactions, and stronger associations with cancer outcomes.

Source: Nucleic Acids Research | GitHub

Topology-based machine learning analysis of chromatin structure for cell-type classification using multiplexed chromatin tracing data

I developed machine learning models to classify cell types from multiplexed chromatin tracing data, with a focus on feature design, model development, evaluation, and biological interpretation. I applied a topology-based representation of chromatin structure using Delaunay tessellation and constructed features from thousands of spatial topological units. Using these features, I trained and evaluated random forest classifiers on mouse cortex chromatin tracing data. The models achieved an overall accuracy of ~0.75 for distinguishing major cell types, compared with ~0.5 accuracy from random controls. Pairwise classification performance further revealed clustering patterns consistent with known major cortical cell classes.

In this project, I designed and implemented the full analysis pipeline, including data preprocessing and curation, coordinate imputation, feature engineering, machine learning model training, performance evaluation, and interpretation of results. This work demonstrates the feasibility of applying machine learning and topology-based feature design to extract biologically meaningful patterns from spatial chromatin organization data.
